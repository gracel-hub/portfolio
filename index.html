Consulting Deliverable · 2020 Biodiversity Risk Framework for Agricultural Lending
+

A due diligence framework developed for ING Group's wholesale lending practice to help risk analysts assess biodiversity exposure in agricultural supply chains. No existing tools could translate biodiversity loss into banking risk metrics at the time, so the team built one from scratch.

Key Findings

  • Five commodities account for the majority of agricultural biodiversity risk: beef/dairy, soy, palm oil, timber, and rubber.
  • Brazil's soy production carries the highest risk — 20% of exports to the EU are linked to illegally deforested areas. The Amazon Soy Moratorium doesn't cover the Cerrado, where deforestation continues.
  • Traceability is the most practical risk signal: if a company cannot trace commodity origin, that alone warrants enhanced due diligence.
  • No single global standard covers all five sectors — analysts must triangulate across multiple certification bodies depending on the commodity.
  • This type of work now sits under the emerging TNFD framework, which formalizes nature-related financial disclosure for institutions.

Key Resources Referenced

  • IBAT — Integrated Biodiversity Assessment Tool
  • ENCORE — mapping sector dependencies on ecosystem services
  • Trase.Earth — supply chain traceability data
  • WWF 2050 Criteria, RSPO (palm oil), FSC (timber), GRSB (beef)
  • Brazil's Rural Environmental Registry (CAR) — baseline for Forest Code compliance
  • CITES Appendix I species listings for timber transactions
Term Paper · 2021 Water Privatization and Markets: Practical or Idealistic?
+

A case study of Chile's 1981 Water Code — one of the world's most cited examples of free-market water governance. The paper argues that while the Code helped fuel Chile's agricultural export economy, it failed to create a true functioning market. Instead it enabled water monopolization, marginalized small farmers, and contributed to a human rights crisis in water-scarce regions. The lesson: markets require institutional infrastructure, not just property rights.

Capstone Research · 2022 What is Coral Worth to Monroe County, Florida?
+

One of my contributions to a Columbia University capstone project for the Coral Restoration Foundation, was researching and quanitifying the economic value of coral reef coverage to Monroe County. The findings showed how reef health directly underpins the two industries that drive the local economy: tourism ($2.5B in annual spending, 43% of all local jobs) and commercial fishing ($63M/yr in landings, led by spiny lobsters that depend on reef habitat for survival). The work connects environmental science to financial impact in concrete terms.
